# Resonance Accessibility Conditions

Resonance accessibility conditions describe the operational requirements under which resonance-window formation and retained synthesis accessibility become dynamically possible in nonlinear open dissipative dynamic systems.

Within the framework, resonance accessibility is not treated as unrestricted synchronization capability.

Operational accessibility depends on preservation of endogenous structural coherence, positive structural balance, coherent accumulation, endogenous drift conditions, and recursive continuity stability.

# Fundamental Accessibility Condition

Resonance accessibility remains operationally possible only while:

`C(t) > P(t)`

over sufficiently preserved operational intervals.

where:

- C(t) — general endogenous structural coherence parameter;

- P(t) — destabilizing structural pressure, fragmentation pressure, structural load, or dissipative maintenance cost.

Operationally this means:

- endogenous structural coherence and regenerative support exceed destabilizing structural pressure;

- retained continuity remains dynamically viable;

- endogenous instability remains bounded;

- synthesis accessibility remains operationally available.

This condition must not be reduced to phase synchronization alone.

Phase synchronization may support resonance accessibility, but real dynamic stability remains governed by:

`C(t) > P(t)`

# Coherent Accumulation Requirement

Resonance accessibility additionally requires:

`Θ_N ≥ Θ_crit`

where:

- Θ_N — accumulated positive structural work;

- Θ_crit — minimum coherent accumulation threshold required for operational resonance accessibility.

Within the framework:

- temporary synchronization alone is insufficient;

- resonance accessibility requires retained constructive accumulation over operational time;

- accumulated positive structural work must be evaluated across completed operational intervals, not isolated instantaneous states.

# Dynamic Accessibility Factors

Operational resonance accessibility depends on:

- endogenous drift velocity;

- perturbation sensitivity;

- recursive continuity stability;

- attractor topology;

- oscillatory phase-synchronization / coherence-support stability;

- and retained operational accessibility.

Operationally this means:

- resonance accessibility evolves dynamically rather than remaining static.

# Relation to Endogenous Drift

Near excessive endogenous drift:

- resonance accessibility compresses;

- stabilization accessibility decreases;

- perturbation sensitivity increases;

- bifurcation accessibility expands.

Near stable drift conditions:

- resonance accessibility broadens;

- coherent accumulation stabilizes;

- synthesis accessibility increases.

# Oscillatory Accessibility Conditions

Near operational resonance regions:

- oscillatory phase synchronization may stabilize coherent accumulation;

- cyclic operational continuity may emerge;

- retained synthesis accessibility may intensify.

Operationally this corresponds to:

- increased operational accessibility of recursive structural self-organization.

However:

- oscillatory phase synchronization is not identical to endogenous structural coherence;

- R(t), if used, remains a synchronization / coherence-support indicator;

- retained synthesis requires accumulated positive structural work and preservation of `C(t) > P(t)` over operational time.

# Recursive Accessibility Dynamics

Within recursively inherited synthesis cycles:

- resonance accessibility evolves recursively;

- inherited attractor topology modifies future accessibility;

- recursive instability accumulation changes future resonance conditions.

Therefore:

- resonance accessibility becomes history-dependent.

Recursive inheritance does not mean mechanical repetition.

It means that prior retained synthesis cycles, attractor topology, and accumulated instability gradients modify future resonance-window accessibility.

# Resonance Compression Near Criticality

Near critical operational regimes:

- resonance-window accessibility intervals narrow;

- phase-synchronization stability weakens;

- coherent accumulation destabilizes;

- stabilization accessibility compresses.

Operationally this means:

- delayed stabilization reduces resonance accessibility.

Near the critical boundary:

`C(t) ≈ P(t)`

small parameter changes may produce disproportionate structural consequences.

This is the EDC-sensitive region where resonance accessibility becomes highly dependent on endogenous drift quality.

# Accessibility Boundaries

Resonance accessibility remains bounded by:

- preservation of endogenous structural coherence;

- positive structural balance preservation;

- coherent accumulation stability;

- recursive continuity accessibility;

- perturbation tolerance limits;

- and retained-domain accessibility.

Operationally this means:

- resonance windows represent dynamically constrained operational accessibility regions rather than unrestricted synchronization states.

A resonance window is not automatically positive.

Its qualitative sign depends on the direction and quality of endogenous drift.

A resonance window may therefore become:

- a synthesis window;

- a reorganization window;

- a degradation window;

- or a collapse window.

# Operational Interpretation

Within the framework, resonance accessibility conditions represent:

- operational constraints governing resonance-window formation;

- dynamic accessibility of synthesis continuity;

- phase-synchronization-supported structural self-organization accessibility;

- and operational accessibility limits of resonance-driven phase-transition dynamics in nonlinear open dissipative dynamic systems.

They remain governed by the core distinction:

- phase synchronization may support resonance accessibility;

- resonance windows may open transition possibilities;

- but real dynamic stability requires endogenous structural coherence to remain stronger than destabilizing pressure:

`C(t) > P(t)`
