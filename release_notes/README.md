# Canonical Stability Condition

The core stability condition remains:

`C(t) > P(t)`

where:

- C(t) — general endogenous structural coherence parameter;

- P(t) — destabilizing structural pressure, fragmentation pressure, structural load, or dissipative maintenance cost.

This condition defines real dynamic stability over operational time.

It must not be replaced by:

- formal structural existence;

- external form;

- temporary synchronization;

- phase alignment;

- instantaneous positive balance;

- or forced organization.

If:

`C(t) ≤ P(t)`

then real dynamic stability is disrupted, even if the system still appears externally structured.

# Structural Balance Clarification

Formal structural balance is described by:

`Δ(t) = S(t) − P(t) − D(t)`

where:

- S(t) — synthesis and positive structural work;

- P(t) — destabilizing structural pressure, fragmentation pressure, structural load, or dissipative maintenance cost;

- D(t) — irreversible structural losses, dissipation, or degradation.

Important distinction:

`Δ(t) > 0`

supports formal structural existence.

But it does not by itself prove real dynamic stability.

Real dynamic stability requires:

`C(t) > P(t)`

This distinction is canonical across the repository.

# C(t) Clarification

C(t) is defined as:

- general endogenous structural coherence parameter.

C(t) is not:

- simple synchronization;

- phase alignment alone;

- R(t);

- Δ(t);

- Θ_N;

- regeneration itself;

- total structural balance;

- accumulated positive structural work;

- external structural form.

Regeneration may support C(t), but regeneration is not identical to C(t).

C(t) expresses the general endogenous coherence of internal self-organization processes that determines real dynamic stability over operational time.

# Reduced EDC C(t) Clarification

In the reduced EDC critical equation:

`dC/dt = rC − C³`

C(t) represents the reduced endogenous structural coherence variable near the critical regime.

It must not be described as total structural balance.

It must not be confused with Δ(t), Θ_N, or simple synchronization.

# Phase Synchronization Clarification

The framework uses the term phase synchronization only where oscillatory or phase-based dynamics are explicitly involved.

Phase synchronization may support:

- resonance-window accessibility;

- coherence-support dynamics.

But:

`R(t) ≠ C(t)`

where:

- R(t) — synchronization / coherence-support indicator;

- C(t) — general endogenous structural coherence parameter.

Phase synchronization does not prove:

- retained synthesis;

- structural integrity;

- real dynamic stability;

- post-forcing persistence.

The primary condition remains:

`C(t) > P(t)`

# Resonance Clarification

Resonance is defined as a dynamic operational process.

It is not:

- simple amplification;

- arbitrary control over matter;

- mere frequency coincidence;

- isolated phase synchronization;

- guaranteed synthesis;

- guaranteed stabilization.

A resonance window is an admissible operational region where transformation becomes dynamically accessible.

A resonance window may become:

- a synthesis window;

- a reorganization window;

- a degradation window;

- or a collapse window.

Therefore:

a resonance window is not automatically positive.

Its qualitative sign depends on the qualitative characteristics of endogenous drift, retained coherence, attractor topology, and accumulated positive structural work.

# Retention Clarification

Retention means preservation of operational organization after reduction or removal of external forcing.

A retained synthesis cycle must remain inside:

`Ω_ret ⊆ Ω(t)`

after external forcing is reduced or removed.

External forcing may support entry into an admissible resonance region.

But external forcing does not prove retained synthesis.

Retention begins only when endogenous dynamics preserve organization without continuous external support.

# Recursive Inheritance

Recursive inheritance is represented by:

`Q(n+1) = Φ(Q(n), D(n), R(n), A(n), E(n))`

where:

- Q(n) — qualitative characteristics of the current retained or degraded cycle;

- Q(n+1) — qualitative characteristics of the subsequent cycle;

- D(n) — accumulated dissipative inheritance;

- R(n) — synchronization / coherence-support configuration;

- A(n) — attractor topology;

- E(n) — environmental interaction gradient;

- Φ — recursive endogenous synthesis operator.

Recursive inheritance does not mean mechanical repetition.

It means that prior retained or degraded regimes modify future accessibility conditions.

# Scaling Law Status

The current scaling law is:

`t_delay ~ v^(−1/3)`

where:

- t_delay — characteristic critical delay;

- v — endogenous drift velocity.

When:

`v = μP`

the scaling may be written as:

`t_delay ~ (μP)^(−1/3)`

This law describes compression of stabilization accessibility near the reduced cubic critical regime.

It does not replace the core stability condition:

`C(t) > P(t)`

The scaling law is valid under the stated local assumptions:

- cubic saturation dominates;

- endogenous drift is locally smooth;

- r(t) ≈ vt;

- the system remains nonlinear, open, dissipative, and dynamically evolving;

- higher-order nonlinearities remain subdominant in the analyzed regime.
