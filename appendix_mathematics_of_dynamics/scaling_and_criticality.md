# Canonical Critical Form and Scaling Law

This appendix describes the canonical critical reduction and the delay scaling law of endogenous dynamic criticality.

Scaling is treated as a consequence of endogenous drift near a critical regime.

---

# Critical Dynamic Regime

Near the critical regime, endogenous structural coherence dynamics are represented as:

    dC/dt = rC − C³

where:

- C(t) — reduced endogenous structural coherence variable near the critical regime;
- r(t) — endogenous control parameter.

The cubic term represents nonlinear saturation of endogenous structural coherence growth.

C(t) is not total structural balance.

C(t) is not identical to Δ(t).

C(t) is not identical to accumulated positive structural work.

C(t) represents the reduced critical coherence variable within the local EDC regime.

---

# Endogenous Drift

Near criticality, the endogenous control parameter evolves approximately as:

    r(t) ≈ vt

where:

- v — endogenous drift velocity toward the bifurcation region.

Within the EDS / EDC framework:

    v = μP

where:

- μ — coupling coefficient;
- P — structural load, destabilizing pressure, fragmentation pressure, or dissipative maintenance cost.

Substitution gives:

    dC/dt = vtC − C³

Operationally:

- endogenous drift moves the system toward or away from the critical regime;
- increasing destabilizing pressure may increase effective drift velocity;
- accelerated drift compresses available stabilization time.

---

# Rescaling

Introduce dimensionless variables:

    C = v^(1/3)y

    t = v^(−1/3)τ

Then:

    dC/dt = v^(2/3) dy/dτ

and:

    vtC − C³ = v^(2/3)(τy − y³)

Therefore:

    v^(2/3) dy/dτ = v^(2/3)(τy − y³)

Dividing by:

    v^(2/3)

gives:

    dy/dτ = τy − y³

This is the canonical parameter-reduced form of the critical regime.

---

# Scaling Law

The characteristic transition delay follows:

    t_delay ~ v^(−1/3)

Since:

    v = μP

the delay scaling can also be written as:

    t_delay ~ (μP)^(−1/3)

The exponent:

    −1/3

emerges from the cubic saturation structure and the smooth endogenous drift approximation.

It is not inserted as an external assumption.

---

# Operational Meaning

The scaling law means that the available stabilization time decreases nonlinearly as endogenous drift velocity increases.

Operationally:

- slower drift increases the time available for adaptive stabilization;
- faster drift compresses the resonance-window accessibility interval;
- delayed correction increases the required stabilization resource;
- excessive drift increases the probability of destructive bifurcation trajectories.

The scaling law describes compression of stabilization accessibility near criticality.

It does not by itself determine whether the transition becomes constructive or destructive.

The qualitative sign of the transition depends on the direction and qualitative characteristics of endogenous drift.

---

# Relation to Structural Balance

The scaling law remains operationally constrained by:

    C(t) > P(t)

where:

- C(t) — general endogenous structural coherence parameter;
- P(t) — destabilizing structural pressure, fragmentation pressure, structural load, or dissipative maintenance cost.

If positive structural balance and endogenous structural coherence are preserved:

- stabilization remains accessible;
- resonance-window traversal remains operationally possible;
- retained synthesis-cycle formation remains viable.

If endogenous structural coherence weakens relative to destabilizing pressure:

- drift accelerates;
- stabilization time decreases;
- transition cost increases;
- bifurcation trajectories become less controllable.

This condition must not be reduced to formal structural existence alone.

A system may preserve external structure while real dynamic stability is already weakening.

Real dynamic stability over operational time remains governed by:

    C(t) > P(t)

---

# Recursive Interpretation

In recursive synthesis dynamics, scaling affects not only the current transition.

It also influences:

- qualitative characteristics of the retained synthesis cycle;
- configuration of subsequent resonance windows;
- inherited drift conditions;
- future stabilization accessibility;
- qualitative characteristics of subsequent synthesis cycles.

Therefore:

    the cost and qualitative characteristics of the next cycle depend on the drift velocity and endogenous structural coherence of the current cycle.

The system does not inherit only a numerical delay.

It inherits qualitative characteristics of the current retained or degraded regime, including:

- direction of endogenous drift;
- drift velocity;
- attractor deformation;
- resonance-window accessibility;
- perturbation sensitivity;
- degree of retained coherence or fragmentation;
- stabilization accessibility;
- and accessibility of subsequent retained operational domains.

---

# Universality Domain

This scaling form applies under the following conditions:

- cubic saturation dominates nonlinear dynamics;
- the control parameter evolves smoothly near criticality;
- endogenous drift can be locally approximated as r(t) ≈ vt;
- the system remains nonlinear, open, dissipative, and dynamically evolving;
- higher-order nonlinearities remain subdominant within the analyzed local regime.

Outside this domain, alternative scaling behavior may arise.

Therefore:

    t_delay ~ v^(−1/3)

is a scaling law of the reduced cubic EDC regime,

not an unrestricted universal law for every possible nonlinear system.

---

# Relation to Resonance-Window Accessibility

Near criticality:

    C(t) ≈ P(t)

resonance-window accessibility becomes compressed.

Operationally:

- stabilization intervals narrow;
- perturbation sensitivity increases;
- retained synthesis accessibility weakens;
- bifurcation accessibility expands.

If endogenous structural coherence remains stronger than destabilizing pressure:

    C(t) > P(t)

then resonance-window traversal may support retained synthesis or reorganization.

If endogenous structural coherence falls below destabilizing pressure:

    C(t) ≤ P(t)

then the same transition region may become a degradation or collapse pathway.

A resonance window is therefore not automatically positive.

Its qualitative sign depends on the qualitative characteristics of endogenous drift and retained structural coherence.

---

# Operational Interpretation

Within the framework, scaling law represents:

- temporal compression of stabilization accessibility;
- nonlinear cost growth near criticality;
- delay dynamics of phase-transition development;
- operational dependence of future synthesis cycles on current drift conditions;
- and inheritance of qualitative characteristics of drift-dependent transition accessibility.

The scaling law explains why delayed correction becomes increasingly costly near criticality.

As endogenous drift accelerates, the available operational window for stabilization compresses according to:

    t_delay ~ v^(−1/3)

The retained synthesis outcome still depends on whether endogenous structural coherence remains stronger than destabilizing pressure:

    C(t) > P(t)

If this condition is preserved or restored, critical delay may become a stabilization interval.

If this condition fails, the delay becomes the narrowing path toward bifurcation, fragmentation, degradation, or collapse.
