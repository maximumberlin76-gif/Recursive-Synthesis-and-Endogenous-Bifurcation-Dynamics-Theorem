# Canonical Critical Form and Scaling Law

This appendix describes the canonical critical reduction and the scaling behavior of endogenous dynamic criticality in nonlinear open dissipative dynamic systems.

Within the framework, scaling behavior emerges from endogenous drift near critical operational regimes.

# Critical Dynamic Regime

Near criticality, endogenous structural coherence dynamics are approximated by:

`dC/dt = vtC − C³`

where:

- C(t) — reduced endogenous structural coherence variable near the critical regime;

- v — endogenous drift velocity toward the bifurcation region.

The cubic term represents nonlinear saturation of endogenous structural coherence growth.

C(t) is not accumulated structural balance.

C(t) is not identical to Δ(t).

C(t) represents the reduced critical coherence variable in the local EDC regime.

# Endogenous Drift Approximation

Near the critical regime:

`r(t) ≈ vt`

where:

- r(t) — endogenous control parameter;

- v — endogenous drift velocity.

Within the framework:

`v = μP`

where:

- μ — coupling coefficient;

- P — structural load, destabilizing pressure, fragmentation pressure, or dissipative maintenance cost.

Substitution produces:

`dC/dt = vtC − C³`

Operationally:

- endogenous drift moves the system toward or away from the critical regime;

- increasing destabilizing pressure may increase effective drift velocity;

- accelerated drift compresses available stabilization time.

# Canonical Rescaling

Introduce rescaled variables:

`C = v^(1/3)y`

`t = v^(−1/3)τ`

Then:

`dC/dt = v^(2/3) dy/dτ`

and:

`vtC − C³ = v^(2/3)(τy − y³)`

Therefore:

`v^(2/3) dy/dτ = v^(2/3)(τy − y³)`

Dividing by:

`v^(2/3)`

gives the canonical critical form:

`dy/dτ = τy − y³`

Operationally this represents the parameter-reduced structure of the local cubic critical regime.

# Scaling Law

The characteristic transition delay follows:

`t_delay ~ v^(−1/3)`

Operationally this means:

- increasing endogenous drift velocity reduces stabilization accessibility time;

- decreasing drift velocity increases adaptive stabilization accessibility.

The exponent:

`−1/3`

emerges from the cubic saturation structure and the smooth endogenous drift approximation.

It is not inserted as an external assumption.

# Structural Interpretation

Within the framework:

- slow endogenous drift supports retained synthesis accessibility;

- accelerated drift compresses resonance-window accessibility;

- excessive drift increases bifurcation sensitivity;

- delayed stabilization increases operational instability.

Operationally this means:

- stabilization cost grows nonlinearly near criticality.

The scaling law describes how much operational time remains available for stabilization as the system approaches the critical regime.

# Recursive Scaling Effects

Scaling behavior influences not only the current transition regime.

It also modifies:

- future resonance-window accessibility;

- recursive synthesis continuity;

- inherited drift conditions;

- stabilization accessibility of subsequent synthesis cycles.

Therefore:

- current drift conditions influence future operational accessibility.

Within recursively inherited synthesis cycles, the system does not inherit a simple numerical delay alone.

It inherits qualitative characteristics of the retained regime, including:

- drift direction;

- drift velocity;

- attractor deformation;

- resonance-window accessibility;

- perturbation sensitivity;

- and accessibility of subsequent retained operational domains.

# Relation to Structural Balance

Scaling accessibility remains operationally constrained by:

`C(t) > P(t)`

where:

- C(t) — general endogenous structural coherence parameter;

- P(t) — destabilizing structural pressure, fragmentation pressure, structural load, or dissipative maintenance cost.

If positive structural balance and endogenous structural coherence remain preserved:

- retained synthesis continuity remains viable;

- stabilization accessibility remains operationally available.

If structural imbalance dominates:

- drift accelerates;

- stabilization intervals compress;

- destructive bifurcation accessibility increases.

This condition must not be reduced to formal structural existence alone.

Formal structural existence may persist while real dynamic stability is already disrupted.

Real dynamic stability over time remains governed by:

`C(t) > P(t)`

# Universality Conditions

This scaling form applies when:

- nonlinear cubic saturation dominates;

- endogenous drift remains locally smooth;

- the control parameter evolves near criticality;

- the system remains nonlinear, open, dissipative, and dynamically evolving;

- higher-order nonlinearities remain subdominant within the analyzed local regime.

Outside these conditions, alternative scaling behavior may emerge.

Therefore:

`t_delay ~ v^(−1/3)`

is a scaling law of the reduced cubic EDC regime,

not an unrestricted universal law for every possible nonlinear system.

# Operational Interpretation

Within the framework, canonical scaling law represents:

- nonlinear compression of stabilization accessibility;

- temporal dynamics of critical transition development;

- recursive dependence of future synthesis accessibility on current drift conditions;

- operational scaling behavior near endogenous critical regimes;

- and inheritance of qualitative characteristics of drift-dependent transition accessibility.

The scaling law explains why delayed correction becomes increasingly costly near criticality.

As endogenous drift accelerates, the available operational window for stabilization compresses according to:

`t_delay ~ v^(−1/3)`

The retained synthesis outcome still depends on whether endogenous structural coherence remains stronger than destabilizing pressure:

`C(t) > P(t)`
