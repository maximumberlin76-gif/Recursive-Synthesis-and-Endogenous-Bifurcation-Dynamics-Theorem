# Retention Accessibility and Boundary Dynamics

Retention accessibility describes the operational ability of a nonlinear open dissipative dynamic system to preserve retained synthesis continuity within dynamically stable operational boundaries.

Within the framework, retained operational boundaries are not static.

Boundary structure evolves recursively through endogenous drift, attractor deformation, structural imbalance accumulation, qualitative characteristics of prior retained regimes, and recursive continuity propagation.

# Retained Boundary Structure

Let:

`Ω_ret`

represent a retained operational domain.

The retained boundary:

`∂Ω_ret`

defines the operational accessibility limit separating:

- retained continuity regions;

- destabilized operational regions;

- bifurcation-accessible trajectories.

Operationally this means:

- retained synthesis accessibility exists only within dynamically preserved operational boundaries.

A retained boundary is not merely a geometric limit.

It represents the operational threshold at which trajectories either remain inside retained continuity or escape toward instability, reorganization, degradation, or collapse.

# Boundary Accessibility

Retention accessibility remains viable only while operational trajectories remain bounded within:

`Ω_ret`

Operationally this requires:

`C(t) > P(t)`

where:

- C(t) — general endogenous structural coherence parameter;

- P(t) — destabilizing structural pressure, fragmentation pressure, structural load, or dissipative maintenance cost.

If positive structural balance and endogenous structural coherence remain preserved:

- retained continuity remains accessible;

- boundary stability remains viable;

- recursive continuity persists.

If structural imbalance dominates:

- retained boundaries destabilize;

- accessibility compresses;

- escape probability increases.

This condition must not be reduced to formal structural existence alone.

A system may preserve external structure while its retained boundary is already losing operational stability.

Real dynamic stability remains governed by:

`C(t) > P(t)`

# Boundary Compression Near Criticality

Near critical operational regimes:

- retained accessibility intervals narrow;

- perturbation sensitivity increases;

- stabilization accessibility decreases;

- recursive instability accumulation intensifies.

Operationally this means:

- smaller perturbations may destabilize retained continuity;

- operational escape trajectories become increasingly accessible.

Near the EDC-sensitive boundary:

`C(t) ≈ P(t)`

the retained boundary becomes increasingly fragile.

Small changes in drift, perturbation load, or attractor deformation may push trajectories toward boundary escape.

# Recursive Boundary Evolution

Within recursively inherited synthesis cycles:

- retained boundaries evolve dynamically;

- inherited attractor deformation modifies future accessibility;

- recursive drift propagation changes future stabilization conditions;

- qualitative characteristics of prior retained regimes shape future boundary stability.

Therefore:

- retention accessibility becomes recursively history-dependent;

- prior continuity influences future boundary stability.

The system does not inherit only the fact of prior retention.

It inherits qualitative characteristics of the retained or degraded regime, including:

- boundary stability;

- boundary sharpness;

- perturbation tolerance;

- attractor deformation;

- basin accessibility;

- resonance-window accessibility;

- direction of endogenous drift;

- degree of retained coherence or fragmentation;

- and accessibility of subsequent retained operational domains.

# Escape Dynamics

If retained accessibility collapses:

- operational trajectories may escape retained continuity regions;

- recursive instability propagation accelerates;

- bifurcation accessibility expands;

- structural fragmentation increases.

Operationally this corresponds to:

- collapse of retained synthesis continuity.

Escape from `Ω_ret` does not necessarily mean immediate disappearance of external structure.

It means that the system no longer remains inside the retained operational domain that preserves real dynamic stability over time.

After escape, the system may enter:

- reorganization;

- degradation;

- collapse;

- or a different operational regime.

The qualitative sign of this transition depends on the direction and qualitative characteristics of endogenous drift.

# Relation to Resonance Accessibility

Retention accessibility remains dynamically connected to resonance accessibility.

Within stable retained domains:

- resonance-window accessibility remains viable;

- synthesis-cycle continuation remains operationally accessible;

- recursive stabilization remains dynamically possible.

Near destabilized boundaries:

- resonance accessibility compresses;

- operational instability increases;

- retained continuity weakens.

A resonance window gives access.

Accumulated positive structural work gives admission.

Retention accessibility determines whether the trajectory remains inside the retained operational domain.

Thus:

- resonance-window entry alone is insufficient;

- temporary phase synchronization is insufficient;

- retained continuity requires preservation of `C(t) > P(t)` and bounded trajectories inside `Ω_ret`.

# Attractor Boundary Dynamics

Boundary dynamics remain linked to attractor topology.

Stable attractor continuity supports:

- bounded operational trajectories;

- retained accessibility preservation;

- recursive continuity stabilization.

Attractor deformation may weaken retained accessibility and increase destabilization probability.

The qualitative characteristics of attractor topology influence boundary behavior.

These qualitative characteristics may include:

- attractor stability;

- local eigenvalue configuration;

- basin size;

- perturbation sensitivity;

- forward invariance;

- resonance-window accessibility;

- and capacity for subsequent synthesis-cycle formation.

If attractor deformation pushes trajectories toward `∂Ω_ret`, retained accessibility compresses.

If attractor topology supports bounded convergence, retained accessibility remains operationally viable.

# Forward Invariance and Boundary Preservation

Retention accessibility requires forward invariance of the retained operational domain.

Operationally:

`x(t₀) ∈ Ω_ret`

must imply:

`x(t) ∈ Ω_ret`

for:

`t ≥ t₀`

under nominal endogenous dynamics.

If trajectories escape the retained boundary without external disruption, the retained regime is not operationally stable.

Forward invariance therefore acts as the boundary-preservation condition of retained synthesis.

# Operational Interpretation

Within the framework, retention accessibility and boundary dynamics represent:

- operational limits of retained synthesis continuity;

- recursive evolution of accessibility boundaries;

- destabilization accessibility near critical regimes;

- dynamic preservation of retained operational continuity in nonlinear open dissipative dynamic systems;

- and inheritance of qualitative characteristics that shape future boundary stability.

Retention accessibility is therefore not static containment.

It is the dynamic ability of a retained operational domain to keep trajectories bounded inside `Ω_ret` while endogenous structural coherence remains stronger than destabilizing pressure:

`C(t) > P(t)`
