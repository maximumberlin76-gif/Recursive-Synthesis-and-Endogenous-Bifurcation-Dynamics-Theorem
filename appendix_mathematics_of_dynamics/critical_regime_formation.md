# Critical Regime Formation

Critical regime formation describes the endogenous transition of a nonlinear open dissipative dynamic system toward a bifurcation-accessible operational state.

Within the framework, criticality is not treated as an instantaneous event.

Criticality emerges through recursively accumulated structural imbalance, endogenous drift deformation, degradation of endogenous structural coherence, and progressive destabilization of retained operational continuity.

# Operational Transition Toward Criticality

Operationally, the system approaches criticality when:

`C(t) → P(t)`

where:

- C(t) — general endogenous structural coherence parameter;

- P(t) — destabilizing structural pressure, fragmentation pressure, structural load, or dissipative maintenance cost.

This corresponds to the progressive reduction of the margin between endogenous structural coherence and destabilizing pressure over operational time.

Near this regime:

- stabilization accessibility decreases;

- endogenous drift accelerates;

- structural sensitivity increases;

- recursive instability accumulation intensifies.

This condition must not be reduced to formal structural balance alone.

A system may preserve formal structural existence while real dynamic stability is already approaching disruption.

Real dynamic stability remains governed by:

`C(t) > P(t)`

# Dynamic Critical Regime

Near the critical region, the operational dynamics may be approximated as:

`dC/dt = rC − C³`

where:

- C — reduced endogenous structural coherence variable near the critical regime;

- r — endogenous control parameter.

Within the framework:

- positive r may support growth or retention of the coherence variable within the reduced critical regime;

- negative r suppresses retained coherence and favors decay toward lower-coherence states;

- criticality emerges near the operational transition boundary where endogenous structural coherence approaches destabilizing pressure.

The cubic term represents nonlinear saturation.

It prevents unbounded growth of the reduced coherence variable inside the local critical model.

# Endogenous Drift Formation

The endogenous control parameter evolves dynamically:

`dr/dt = μ(P − C)`

where:

- μ — coupling coefficient between structural imbalance and critical drift formation;

- P − C — local imbalance between destabilizing pressure and retained endogenous structural coherence.

Operationally this means:

- increasing structural imbalance accelerates endogenous drift toward criticality;

- prolonged imbalance recursively destabilizes retained operational continuity;

- the system approaches a regime where small parameter changes may produce disproportionate structural consequences.

# Recursive Criticality Accumulation

Criticality is recursively accumulated through:

- retained structural imbalance;

- inherited instability gradients;

- recursive attractor deformation;

- endogenous drift propagation;

- degradation of retained endogenous structural coherence.

Therefore:

- criticality is history-dependent;

- prior synthesis cycles influence future bifurcation accessibility;

- recursive inheritance modifies future critical behavior;

- inherited qualitative characteristics of prior regimes shape the next critical transition.

The system does not inherit only a numerical state.

It inherits qualitative characteristics of the retained or degraded regime, including:

- direction of endogenous drift;

- attractor deformation;

- resonance-window accessibility;

- perturbation sensitivity;

- degree of retained coherence or fragmentation;

- and accessibility of subsequent retained operational domains.

# Resonance-Window Compression

Near criticality:

- resonance-window accessibility compresses;

- stabilization intervals decrease;

- operational flexibility weakens;

- structural sensitivity increases.

Operationally this means:

- delayed stabilization increases destabilization probability;

- corrective operational cost increases nonlinearly near critical regimes.

Near the critical boundary:

`C(t) ≈ P(t)`

resonance windows are not automatically constructive.

Their qualitative sign depends on the direction and qualitative characteristics of endogenous drift.

A critical window may therefore become:

- a synthesis window;

- a reorganization window;

- a degradation window;

- or a collapse window.

# Relation to Eigenvalue Drift

Critical regime formation remains linked to eigenvalue deformation.

Near criticality:

`max(Re(λ_i)) → 0`

Operationally this corresponds to:

- weakening structural stability;

- increasing bifurcation accessibility;

- amplification of perturbation sensitivity.

When the dominant real eigenvalue approaches zero, local restoring capacity weakens.

If it crosses into a positive real part, destabilizing modes may become dynamically accessible.

# Retained Operational Stability

A retained operational regime remains viable only while:

`C(t) > P(t)`

If structural losses and destabilizing pressure dominate over operational time:

- endogenous drift accelerates;

- recursive instability propagates;

- retained synthesis continuity collapses;

- destructive bifurcation trajectories become operationally accessible.

If endogenous structural coherence can be restored above destabilizing pressure:

- stabilization accessibility may remain available;

- resonance-window accessibility may support reorganization;

- retained synthesis continuity may still be preserved.

# Operational Interpretation

Within the framework, critical regime formation represents:

- endogenous destabilization of retained operational continuity;

- recursive accumulation of structural imbalance;

- degradation of endogenous structural coherence;

- operational approach toward bifurcation accessibility;

- compression of stabilization accessibility in nonlinear open dissipative dynamic systems;

- and inheritance of qualitative characteristics shaping future transition paths.

Criticality is therefore not a single point event.

It is an endogenous operational regime formed through drift, imbalance, attractor deformation, and loss of retained coherence.

The core condition remains:

`C(t) > P(t)`

When this condition approaches equality:

`C(t) ≈ P(t)`

the system enters the EDC-sensitive region where bifurcation accessibility increases and stabilization time becomes compressed.
