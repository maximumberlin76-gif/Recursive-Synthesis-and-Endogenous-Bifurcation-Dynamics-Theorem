# Lyapunov Stability of Retained Operational Regimes

Lyapunov stability describes the ability of a system to preserve operational continuity under bounded perturbations.

Within the framework, Lyapunov stability determines whether retained synthesis cycles preserve structural continuity over operational time.

# Local Stability Condition

Consider the nonlinear operational system:

`dx/dt = F(x)`

Let:

`x*`

be a retained operational regime.

The regime is Lyapunov-stable if sufficiently small perturbations:

`δx = x − x*`

remain bounded over time.

Operationally:

- small perturbations must not destroy retained synthesis continuity;

- endogenous structural coherence must remain operationally preserved;

- endogenous drift must remain controllable.

# Lyapunov Function

Let:

`V(x)`

be a positive operational functional satisfying:

`V(x) > 0`

for:

`x ≠ x*`

and:

`V(x*) = 0`

The operational stability condition becomes:

`dV/dt ≤ 0`

Operationally this means:

- the system does not amplify destructive perturbations;

- structural imbalance does not grow uncontrollably;

- retained synthesis cycles preserve continuity.

# Relation to Structural Balance

Within the framework, Lyapunov stability remains operationally constrained by:

`C(t) > P(t)`

where:

- C(t) — general endogenous structural coherence parameter;

- P(t) — destabilizing structural pressure, fragmentation pressure, structural load, or dissipative maintenance cost.

If positive structural balance and endogenous structural coherence collapse:

- perturbation energy increases;

- recursive instability propagates;

- endogenous drift accelerates;

- retained synthesis cycles destabilize.

Therefore:

- preservation of positive structural balance may support Lyapunov stability;

- preservation of endogenous structural coherence is required for real dynamic stability over time;

- prolonged structural imbalance reduces operational stability accessibility.

This condition must not be reduced to local Lyapunov stability alone.

A regime may remain locally bounded under small perturbations while broader endogenous structural coherence is already weakening.

Real dynamic stability remains governed by:

`C(t) > P(t)`

# Retained Operational Regimes

A retained synthesis cycle remains operationally stable only if:

- perturbations remain bounded;

- recursive continuity is preserved;

- attractor deformation remains limited;

- endogenous drift does not exceed operational stability thresholds;

- endogenous structural coherence remains stronger than destabilizing pressure.

Operationally this means:

- the structure preserves continuity after perturbation;

- synthesis accessibility remains operationally available;

- resonance-window continuity remains retained.

A retained operational regime is therefore not defined only by local boundedness.

It also requires preservation of retained-domain accessibility and real dynamic stability over operational time.

# Recursive Stability

Within recursively inherited synthesis cycles:

- perturbations may accumulate over time;

- inherited instability may propagate through attractor topology;

- recursive drift may amplify structural sensitivity;

- qualitative characteristics of prior retained regimes may modify future stability accessibility.

Therefore:

- stability must be evaluated recursively;

- local stability alone is insufficient for long-term operational continuity;

- prior synthesis cycles influence future Lyapunov stability conditions.

The system does not inherit only a stable or unstable state.

It inherits qualitative characteristics of the retained or degraded regime, including:

- perturbation tolerance;

- attractor stability;

- basin accessibility;

- forward invariance of retained domains;

- direction of endogenous drift;

- degree of retained coherence or fragmentation;

- and accessibility of subsequent retained operational domains.

# Lyapunov Stability and Retained Domains

Lyapunov stability remains connected to retained operational domains.

A retained operational domain:

`Ω_ret`

is meaningful only if perturbations do not drive trajectories out of the retained domain under nominal conditions.

Operationally:

- Lyapunov stability supports bounded response;

- forward invariance preserves domain retention;

- basin accessibility determines whether the retained regime is reachable;

- endogenous structural coherence determines whether retained stability persists over operational time.

Thus, Lyapunov stability supports retained synthesis, but does not replace the broader EDS condition:

`C(t) > P(t)`

# Critical Boundary

Near the critical boundary:

`C(t) ≈ P(t)`

Lyapunov stability becomes increasingly fragile.

Operationally:

- perturbation sensitivity increases;

- stabilization accessibility compresses;

- resonance-window accessibility narrows;

- bifurcation accessibility expands.

If endogenous structural coherence is restored above destabilizing pressure:

`C(t) > P(t)`

the retained regime may preserve operational stability.

If not, bounded perturbation response may fail and the system may move toward reorganization, degradation, or collapse.

# Operational Interpretation

Within the framework, Lyapunov stability represents:

- operational continuity of retained synthesis cycles;

- bounded structural response to perturbation;

- preservation of recursive continuity;

- local support for retained-domain stability;

- and resistance of endogenous structural organization to destructive instability growth.

Lyapunov stability is necessary for retained operational regimes, but it is not sufficient by itself.

Real retained dynamic stability requires that endogenous structural coherence remain stronger than destabilizing pressure over operational time:

`C(t) > P(t)`

The inherited qualitative characteristics of prior synthesis cycles determine how strongly future retained regimes resist perturbation, preserve basin accessibility, and remain inside retained operational domains.
