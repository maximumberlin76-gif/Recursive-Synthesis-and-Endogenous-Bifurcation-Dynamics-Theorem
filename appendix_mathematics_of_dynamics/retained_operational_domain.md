# Retained Operational Domain

The retained operational domain represents the dynamically stable operational region in which structural continuity remains preserved after reduction or removal of external forcing.

Within the framework, retained operational domains define the conditions under which synthesis cycles preserve continuity through endogenous operational dynamics.

# Operational Domain Structure

Let:

`Ω(t)`

represent the dynamically accessible operational state-space domain of the system.

A retained operational domain is defined as:

`Ω_ret ⊆ Ω(t)`

where:

- Ω_ret — retained operational continuity region;

- Ω(t) — total dynamically accessible operational domain.

Operationally:

- Ω_ret represents the region in which retained synthesis continuity remains dynamically viable.

A retained operational domain is not merely a geometric subset.

It represents the operational region in which trajectories preserve retained structural continuity under endogenous dynamics.

# Retention Condition

A synthesis cycle is considered operationally retained only if:

`∃ τ > 0`

such that:

`∀ t ≥ t₀ + τ : x(t) ∈ Ω_ret`

after reduction or removal of external forcing.

Operationally this means:

- structural continuity persists without continuous external stabilization;

- recursive operational continuity remains preserved;

- endogenous dynamics sustain retained organization;

- the system does not immediately collapse after forcing reduction.

External forcing may support entry into an admissible resonance window.

It does not by itself prove retained synthesis.

Retention begins only when endogenous dynamics preserve organization inside `Ω_ret`.

# Relation to Structural Balance

Retention accessibility remains operationally constrained by:

`C(t) > P(t)`

where:

- C(t) — general endogenous structural coherence parameter;

- P(t) — destabilizing structural pressure, fragmentation pressure, structural load, or dissipative maintenance cost.

If positive structural balance and endogenous structural coherence remain preserved:

- retained synthesis continuity remains viable;

- operational trajectories remain bounded;

- recursive continuity stabilizes.

If structural imbalance dominates:

- retained continuity destabilizes;

- operational trajectories escape retained accessibility;

- recursive fragmentation increases.

This condition must not be reduced to formal structural existence alone.

A system may preserve external structure while its retained operational domain is already losing stability.

Real dynamic stability remains governed by:

`C(t) > P(t)`

# Dynamic Accessibility

The retained operational domain must remain dynamically accessible.

Operationally this means:

- the retained regime must be physically reachable;

- operational continuity must emerge through admissible system evolution;

- retention cannot represent an unreachable isolated singularity.

A retained operational domain must therefore possess a non-zero basin of attraction.

If `Ω_ret` exists mathematically but cannot be reached through admissible system trajectories, it does not define operational retention.

# Retained Continuity

Within retained operational domains:

- synthesis cycles preserve continuity;

- recursive inheritance remains operationally stable;

- attractor deformation remains bounded;

- endogenous drift remains controllable;

- trajectories remain inside retained-domain boundaries.

Operationally this corresponds to:

- sustained structural self-organization.

Retained continuity requires more than temporary phase alignment or resonance response.

It requires persistence of endogenous structural coherence over operational time.

# Recursive Retention

Within recursively inherited synthesis cycles:

- retained domains may evolve dynamically;

- inherited attractor topology modifies future retention accessibility;

- recursive continuity influences future operational stability;

- qualitative characteristics of prior retained regimes shape future retained-domain accessibility.

Therefore:

- retention becomes history-dependent;

- prior synthesis continuity influences future retained accessibility.

The system does not inherit only the fact that a prior regime was retained.

It inherits qualitative characteristics of the retained regime, including:

- boundary stability of `Ω_ret`;

- basin accessibility;

- forward invariance;

- attractor topology;

- perturbation tolerance;

- resonance-window accessibility;

- direction of endogenous drift;

- degree of retained coherence or fragmentation;

- and accessibility of subsequent retained operational domains.

# Resonance Accessibility

Retention and resonance accessibility remain dynamically connected.

Within stable retained domains:

- resonance-window accessibility remains operationally viable;

- synthesis-cycle continuation remains possible;

- recursive operational continuity stabilizes.

Near destabilized retained domains:

- resonance accessibility compresses;

- synthesis continuity weakens;

- operational instability increases.

A resonance window gives access.

Accumulated positive structural work gives admission.

Retention inside `Ω_ret` proves operational persistence.

# Forward Invariance

A retained operational domain must preserve forward invariance under nominal endogenous dynamics.

Operationally:

`x(t₀) ∈ Ω_ret`

must imply:

`x(t) ∈ Ω_ret`

for:

`t ≥ t₀`

under nominal retained-regime conditions.

If trajectories escape `Ω_ret` without external disruption, the regime is not operationally retained.

Forward invariance therefore acts as a core condition of retained-domain stability.

# Critical Boundary

Near the critical boundary:

`C(t) ≈ P(t)`

retained operational domains become increasingly fragile.

Operationally:

- retained-domain boundaries become more sensitive;

- perturbations more easily drive trajectories outside `Ω_ret`;

- resonance-window accessibility compresses;

- bifurcation accessibility increases.

If endogenous structural coherence is restored above destabilizing pressure:

`C(t) > P(t)`

retention may remain operationally viable.

If not, trajectories may escape the retained domain and move toward reorganization, degradation, or collapse.

# Operational Interpretation

Within the framework, retained operational domains represent:

- dynamically preserved structural continuity;

- operational persistence after external forcing reduction;

- recursive stabilization accessibility;

- retained synthesis continuity in nonlinear open dissipative dynamic systems;

- and inheritance of qualitative characteristics that shape future retained-domain accessibility.

A retained operational domain is therefore not a static container.

It is the operational region in which endogenous structural coherence, attractor topology, forward invariance, and basin accessibility preserve retained synthesis continuity over time.

The core condition remains:

`C(t) > P(t)`
