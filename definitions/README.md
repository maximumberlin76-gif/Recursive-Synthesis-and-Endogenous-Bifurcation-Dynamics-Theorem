# Definitions

This section contains the canonical definitions used in the Recursive Synthesis and Endogenous Bifurcation Dynamics Theorem.

The purpose of this layer is to prevent semantic drift between theorem sections, appendices, operational interpretation, and philosophical interpretation.

All terms in this framework must preserve their defined meaning across the repository.

# System Class

The framework applies to nonlinear open dissipative dynamic systems.

A system belongs to this class if it:

- evolves over time;

- exchanges energy, matter, information, structural influence, or constraints with its environment;

- contains nonlinear feedback, thresholds, amplification, drift, or regime transitions;

- experiences dissipation, degradation, loss, or irreversible structural cost;

- preserves organization only through dynamic maintenance rather than static equilibrium.

The framework does not claim universal applicability to every possible system.

Its operational domain is:

- nonlinear;

- open;

- dissipative;

- dynamic;

- recursively evolving.

# Operational Time — t

`t`

represents operational time.

Operational time is the temporal dimension across which structural balance, endogenous structural coherence, drift, synthesis, dissipation, retention, and recursive inheritance evolve.

# System State — x(t)

`x(t)`

represents the operational state trajectory of the system over time.

It describes the evolving dynamic configuration of the system within its admissible operational domain.

# Operational Domain — Ω(t)

`Ω(t)`

represents the dynamically accessible operational state-space domain of the system.

It contains the region in which system trajectories may evolve under admissible operational conditions.

# Retained Operational Domain — Ω_ret

`Ω_ret`

represents the retained operational subset of the accessible domain.

Formally:

`Ω_ret ⊆ Ω(t)`

Operationally, Ω_ret is the region in which retained synthesis continuity remains dynamically stable after reduction or removal of external forcing.

A retained operational domain must satisfy:

- dynamic accessibility;

- non-zero basin of attraction;

- forward invariance;

- bounded perturbation response;

- retained attractor stability;

- preservation of endogenous structural coherence.

# Synthesis — S(t)

`S(t)`

represents synthesis and positive structural work at time t.

It includes constructive processes that contribute to:

- structural organization;

- regeneration;

- coherent accumulation;

- retained operational continuity;

- formation of new operational regimes;

- reinforcement of endogenous structural coherence.

Constraint:

`S(t) ≥ 0`

# Destabilizing Pressure — P(t)

`P(t)`

represents destabilizing structural pressure.

It may include:

- structural load;

- extraction pressure;

- fragmentation pressure;

- perturbation pressure;

- dissipative maintenance cost;

- overload;

- destabilizing operational burden.

Constraint:

`P(t) ≥ 0`

P(t) is not merely external pressure.

It represents the operational pressure that weakens retained structural continuity and competes against endogenous structural coherence.

# Dissipation — D(t)

`D(t)`

represents irreversible structural losses, degradation, and dissipative cost at time t.

It includes:

- entropy production;

- irreversible loss;

- degradation;

- inefficiency;

- structural decay;

- non-recoverable operational cost.

Constraint:

`D(t) ≥ 0`

# Structural Balance — Δ(t)

`Δ(t)`

represents instantaneous structural balance.

Defined as:

`Δ(t) = S(t) − P(t) − D(t)`

Interpretation:

- Δ(t) > 0 → positive structural formation balance;

- Δ(t) = 0 → quasi-stationary structural balance;

- Δ(t) < 0 → structural degradation or fragmentation.

Important distinction:

`Δ(t) > 0`

supports formal structural existence.

But it does not by itself prove real dynamic stability over time.

Real dynamic stability requires:

`C(t) > P(t)`

# Endogenous Structural Coherence — C(t)

`C(t)`

represents the general endogenous structural coherence parameter.

It determines the level of structural integrity and dynamic stability over operational time.

C(t) describes the internal coherence of the system’s self-organization processes.

It is not:

- simple synchronization;

- phase alignment alone;

- structural balance Δ(t);

- accumulated positive structural work Θ_N;

- external form;

- temporary organization;

- regeneration itself.

Regeneration may support C(t), but regeneration is not identical to C(t).

C(t) expresses the general level of endogenous structural coherence required for real dynamic stability.

The primary stability condition is:

`C(t) > P(t)`

# Primary Stability Condition

The core EDS / recursive synthesis stability condition is:

`C(t) > P(t)`

where:

- C(t) — general endogenous structural coherence;

- P(t) — destabilizing structural pressure.

Operational meaning:

The system remains dynamically stable only while endogenous structural coherence remains stronger than destabilizing pressure.

If:

`C(t) ≤ P(t)`

then real dynamic stability is disrupted, even if external structure remains temporarily visible.

# Formal Existence vs Real Dynamic Stability

Formal structural existence is described by:

`Δ(t) = S(t) − P(t) − D(t)`

Real dynamic stability is described by:

`C(t) > P(t)`

These two conditions must not be merged.

A system may satisfy:

`Δ(t) > 0`

while failing:

`C(t) > P(t)`

This means:

- the structure may still appear externally present;

- but its real dynamic stability over time is already disrupted.

# Accumulated Positive Structural Work — Θ_N

`Θ_N`

represents accumulated positive structural work across N completed operational periods.

It is the accumulated constructive contribution required for synthesis accessibility.

A retained synthesis cycle requires:

`Θ_N ≥ Θ_crit`

where:

- Θ_N — accumulated positive structural work;

- Θ_crit — critical accumulation threshold required for retained synthesis accessibility.

Θ_N prevents temporary excitation or short-lived synchronization from being misclassified as synthesis.

# Critical Accumulation Threshold — Θ_crit

`Θ_crit`

represents the minimum accumulated positive structural work required for retained synthesis accessibility.

If:

`Θ_N < Θ_crit`

then the system may exhibit temporary excitation, resonance response, or phase synchronization, but retained synthesis is not established.

# Resonance

Resonance is a dynamic operational process, not simple amplification.

Within this framework, resonance means an operational condition in which system dynamics become more accessible to coherent structural reorganization under admissible constraints.

Resonance is not:

- arbitrary control over matter;

- simple frequency coincidence;

- mere amplification;

- isolated phase synchronization;

- guaranteed synthesis;

- guaranteed stabilization.

Resonance may support synthesis only if it contributes to:

- accumulated positive structural work;

- retained-domain accessibility;

- preservation of endogenous structural coherence;

- and post-forcing operational persistence.

# Resonance Window

A resonance window is an admissible operational region in which structural transformation becomes dynamically accessible.

A resonance window is not automatically positive.

It may become:

- a synthesis window;

- a reorganization window;

- a degradation window;

- or a collapse window.

The qualitative sign of a resonance window depends on:

- endogenous structural coherence;

- accumulated positive structural work;

- endogenous drift direction;

- attractor topology;

- retained-domain accessibility;

- perturbation sensitivity;

- inherited qualitative characteristics.

A resonance window gives access.

It does not itself prove synthesis.

# Resonance-Window Accessibility

Resonance-window accessibility describes whether the system can enter and use an admissible resonance region.

It depends on:

- C(t) > P(t);

- Θ_N ≥ Θ_crit;

- endogenous drift;

- attractor topology;

- basin accessibility;

- perturbation sensitivity;

- retained-domain stability;

- qualitative characteristics inherited from previous cycles.

# Retention

Retention means preservation of operational organization after reduction or removal of external forcing.

A regime is retained only if:

`x(t) ∈ Ω_ret`

after external forcing is reduced or removed.

Retention is not:

- temporary synchronization;

- forced order;

- externally maintained structure;

- short-lived resonance response.

Retention begins when endogenous dynamics preserve organization without continuous external support.

# Forward Invariance

Forward invariance means that trajectories remain inside the retained operational domain under nominal endogenous dynamics.

Formally:

`x(t₀) ∈ Ω_ret`

implies:

`x(t) ∈ Ω_ret`

for:

`t ≥ t₀`

If trajectories escape Ω_ret without external disruption, the regime is not operationally retained.

# Basin of Attraction

The basin of attraction represents the set of initial operational states from which trajectories converge toward a retained attractor.

A retained operational regime must possess a non-zero basin of attraction.

If a retained domain exists mathematically but cannot be reached through admissible trajectories, it is not operationally meaningful.

# Attractor

An attractor represents the retained dynamic configuration toward which system trajectories converge under endogenous dynamics.

Within this framework, an attractor is not only a mathematical object.

It is the operational memory of retained dynamics.

Attractor topology influences:

- future resonance-window accessibility;

- basin accessibility;

- forward invariance;

- perturbation tolerance;

- bifurcation accessibility;

- qualitative characteristics of subsequent synthesis cycles.

# Attractor Topology

Attractor topology describes the structural organization of attractor dynamics.

It determines:

- convergence behavior;

- accessibility of retained regimes;

- stability of operational trajectories;

- deformation under drift;

- transition accessibility;

- future synthesis-cycle configuration.

Attractor topology may support retained synthesis or contribute to degradation depending on its qualitative characteristics.

# Phase Synchronization

Phase synchronization means alignment of phase, rhythm, or cyclic behavior among oscillatory subsystems.

The term phase synchronization should be used only when oscillatory or phase-based dynamics are explicitly involved.

Phase synchronization may support:

- coherent accumulation;

- resonance-window accessibility;

- cyclic operational continuity;

- coherence-support dynamics.

But phase synchronization is not identical to endogenous structural coherence.

# Synchronization Indicator — R(t)

`R(t)`

represents a synchronization / coherence-support indicator.

It may measure phase-related order, cyclic alignment, or operational synchronization.

Important distinction:

`R(t) ≠ C(t)`

R(t) does not prove:

- real dynamic stability;

- structural integrity;

- retained synthesis;

- endogenous structural coherence;

- post-forcing persistence.

R(t) may support interpretation, but the primary stability condition remains:

`C(t) > P(t)`

# Coherence

Coherence means internally compatible structural organization across system processes.

Within the framework, coherence is broader than synchronization.

Coherence includes:

- compatibility of internal processes;

- retained structural continuity;

- functional contribution to the whole;

- alignment of dynamics, accumulation, and regeneration;

- preservation of structural integrity over time.

Coherence is not merely simultaneous motion or phase alignment.

# Endogenous Drift

Endogenous drift describes internally generated movement of system conditions toward or away from critical regimes.

Near criticality:

`C(t) ≈ P(t)`

the system becomes highly sensitive to small parameter changes.

In the reduced EDC regime:

`dC/dt = rC − C³`

where:

- C(t) — reduced endogenous structural coherence variable near the critical regime;

- r(t) — endogenous control parameter.

Near criticality:

`r(t) ≈ vt`

where:

- v — endogenous drift velocity.

# Reduced Critical Coherence Variable

In the reduced EDC equation:

`dC/dt = rC − C³`

C(t) represents the reduced endogenous structural coherence variable near the critical regime.

It is not:

- total structural balance;

- Δ(t);

- Θ_N;

- simple synchronization;

- accumulated positive structural work.

This reduced variable models local critical coherence dynamics.

# Critical Delay

Critical delay describes the remaining operational interval before bifurcation-accessible transition dynamics become dominant.

In the reduced cubic EDC regime:

`t_delay ~ v^(−1/3)`

where:

- v — endogenous drift velocity.

The scaling law applies within the reduced cubic critical regime.

It is not an unrestricted universal law for every nonlinear system.

# Recursive Inheritance

Recursive inheritance describes how retained or degraded regimes modify subsequent operational cycles.

It is represented by:

`Q(n+1) = Φ(Q(n), D(n), R(n), A(n), E(n))`

where:

- Q(n) — qualitative characteristics of the current regime;

- Q(n+1) — qualitative characteristics of the subsequent regime;

- D(n) — accumulated dissipative inheritance;

- R(n) — synchronization / coherence-support configuration;

- A(n) — attractor topology;

- E(n) — environmental interaction gradient;

- Φ — recursive endogenous synthesis operator.

Recursive inheritance does not mean mechanical repetition.

It means prior regime characteristics modify future accessibility.

# Qualitative Characteristics

The framework uses:

- qualitative characteristics

not:

- quality.

This is mandatory.

Qualitative characteristics are not an abstract evaluation.

They are the inherited regime profile.

They may include:

- direction of endogenous drift;

- drift velocity;

- attractor stability;

- basin accessibility;

- forward invariance of retained domains;

- perturbation sensitivity;

- resonance-window accessibility;

- degree of retained coherence or fragmentation;

- capacity for subsequent accumulation of positive structural work;

- accessibility of subsequent retained operational domains.

# Recursive Synthesis

Recursive synthesis is the process by which retained operational organization becomes a condition for future operational organization.

A retained synthesis cycle passes forward qualitative characteristics that configure subsequent cycles.

Recursive synthesis requires:

- C(t) > P(t);

- Θ_N ≥ Θ_crit;

- retention inside Ω_ret;

- forward invariance;

- non-zero basin accessibility;

- retained attractor topology;

- inheritance of constructive qualitative characteristics.

# Retention Failure

Retention failure occurs when retained synthesis continuity is no longer preserved.

The failure condition is:

`C(t) ≤ P(t)`

over sufficiently prolonged operational intervals.

Operational consequences may include:

- escape from Ω_ret;

- basin compression;

- loss of forward invariance;

- attractor fragmentation;

- resonance-window compression;

- inherited degradation;

- recursive fragmentation.

# Fragmentation

Fragmentation describes the breakdown of retained operational continuity into unstable, partially disconnected, or degraded dynamics.

Fragmentation is not necessarily immediate collapse.

It is loss of retained coherent continuity.

Fragmentation may become recursively inherited through degraded qualitative characteristics.

# Collapse

Collapse means that retained operational organization can no longer be reconstructed within the previous retained domain.

Collapse is stronger than fragmentation.

Fragmentation may precede collapse, but they are not identical.

# Constructive Regime

A constructive regime exists when:

- C(t) > P(t);

- Θ_N ≥ Θ_crit;

- trajectories remain inside Ω_ret;

- forward invariance is preserved;

- basin accessibility remains non-zero;

- qualitative characteristics support subsequent synthesis.

# Degradation Regime

A degradation regime exists when:

- C(t) ≤ P(t);

- retained-domain accessibility weakens;

- basin accessibility compresses;

- attractor topology deforms destructively;

- resonance-window accessibility compresses;

- degraded qualitative characteristics become inherited.

# Canonical Rule

The canonical rule of the framework is:

Do not replace endogenous structural coherence with synchronization.

Do not replace qualitative characteristics with quality.

Do not replace resonance-window accessibility with guaranteed synthesis.

Do not replace formal structural existence with real dynamic stability.

Do not replace retention with externally forced organization.

# Minimal Chain

The minimal operational chain is:

`Δ(t) = S(t) − P(t) − D(t)`

supports formal structural existence.

`C(t) > P(t)`

defines real dynamic stability.

`Θ_N ≥ Θ_crit`

defines accumulated positive structural work sufficient for synthesis accessibility.

`x(t) ∈ Ω_ret`

defines retained operational persistence.

`Q(n+1) = Φ(Q(n), D(n), R(n), A(n), E(n))`

defines recursive inheritance of qualitative characteristics.

# Final Definition

Recursive Synthesis and Endogenous Bifurcation Dynamics describes how retained synthesis cycles transmit qualitative characteristics into subsequent operational cycles, shaping future resonance-window accessibility, attractor topology, endogenous drift, bifurcation dynamics, and retained structural self-organization.

The core condition remains:

`C(t) > P(t)`
